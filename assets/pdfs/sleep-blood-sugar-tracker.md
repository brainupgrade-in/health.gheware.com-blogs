## The Science: How Sleep Affects Blood Sugar

### 1. Insulin Sensitivity & Sleep Deprivation

**Research Finding:** A landmark study published in *Diabetes Care* showed that sleeping only 4-5 hours per night for just one week reduced insulin sensitivity by 25%.

**What happens:**
- Sleep deprivation triggers stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline)
- These hormones signal the liver to release more glucose
- Cells become resistant to insulin's effects
- Blood sugar rises even without eating

### 2. The Cortisol Connection

| Sleep Duration | Cortisol Level | Blood Sugar Impact |
|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 7-9 hours | Normal rhythm | Stable fasting glucose |
| 5-6 hours | 37% higher | +10-20 mg/dL fasting |
| <5 hours | 50%+ higher | +20-40 mg/dL fasting |

### 3. Growth Hormone & Glucose Regulation

- 70% of daily growth hormone is released during deep sleep
- Growth hormone helps regulate glucose metabolism
- Poor sleep = disrupted growth hormone = impaired glucose handling

### 4. Appetite Hormones Gone Wrong

**Ghrelin (Hunger Hormone):**
- Increases by 28% with sleep deprivation
- Triggers cravings for high-carb, sugary foods

**Leptin (Satiety Hormone):**
- Decreases by 18% with poor sleep
- Makes it harder to feel full

**Result:** Sleep-deprived individuals eat an average of 385 extra calories per day, mostly from carbohydrates.

### 5. The Dawn Phenomenon

Between 3-8 AM, your body naturally releases:
- Cortisol
- Glucagon
- Epinephrine

This causes blood sugar to rise in preparation for waking. **Poor sleep amplifies this effect significantly.**

## Optimal Sleep Duration & Timing

### Recommended Sleep by Age (American Diabetes Association)

| Age Group | Recommended Sleep | For Diabetics |
|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| 18-25 years | 7-9 hours | 7.5-8.5 hours |
| 26-64 years | 7-9 hours | 7.5-8.5 hours |
| 65+ years | 7-8 hours | 7-8 hours |

### The "Sweet Spot" for Diabetics

Research from the *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism* found:

- **< 6 hours:** 28% higher risk of poor glycemic control
- **6-7 hours:** 15% higher risk
- **7-8 hours:** Optimal zone âœ“
- **> 9 hours:** 11% higher risk (associated with depression, other issues)

### Timing Matters: Circadian Rhythm

**Best Sleep Schedule for Blood Sugar:**

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 9:00 PM | Begin wind-down routine |
| 10:00-10:30 PM | Ideal bedtime |
| 6:00-7:00 AM | Ideal wake time |
| Same time daily | Consistency is key! |

**Why consistency matters:**
- Irregular sleep schedules increase A1C by 0.5% on average
- "Social jet lag" (weekend sleep changes) disrupts insulin sensitivity

### Your Chronotype Matters

**Morning Person (Early Chronotype):**
- Sleep: 9:30 PM - 5:30 AM
- Peak insulin sensitivity: Morning

**Evening Person (Late Chronotype):**
- Sleep: 11:00 PM - 7:00 AM
- May need to work harder on morning glucose

## Sleep Hygiene Tips for Diabetics

### The SLEEP-WELL Protocol

**S - Schedule Consistency**
- Go to bed and wake at the same time daily
- Even on weekends, stay within 30 minutes of your schedule
- Set two alarms: one for bedtime, one for morning

**L - Light Management**
- Get 20-30 minutes of morning sunlight
- Dim lights 2 hours before bed
- Use blue light filters after sunset
- Make bedroom completely dark (blackout curtains)

**E - Environment Optimization**
- Temperature: 65-68Â°F (18-20Â°C) is ideal
- Humidity: 30-50%
- White noise if needed
- Comfortable, supportive mattress

**E - Exercise Timing**
- Morning/afternoon exercise improves sleep quality
- Finish vigorous exercise 3+ hours before bed
- Light stretching or yoga before bed is beneficial

**P - Pre-bed Routine**
- Stop eating 3 hours before bed (crucial for diabetics!)
- Last caffeine: 2 PM or earlier
- Last alcohol: 3 hours before bed
- 30-minute wind-down routine

**W - Worry Management**
- Journal worries before bed (included in tracker)
- Practice 4-7-8 breathing
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Blood sugar check before bed (reduces anxiety)

**E - Electronics Curfew**
- No screens 1 hour before bed
- No phones in bedroom
- Use traditional alarm clock

**L - Limit Disruptions**
- Use bathroom before bed
- Keep water by bed (but limit evening fluids)
- Address snoring/breathing issues

**L - Last Blood Sugar Check**
- Check glucose 30 minutes before bed
- Target: 100-150 mg/dL for most adults
- Prevents overnight hypo/hyperglycemia anxiety

### Bedtime Blood Sugar Targets

| Bedtime Reading | Action |
|-----------------|--------|
| < 100 mg/dL | Small snack (15-20g carbs + protein) |
| 100-150 mg/dL | Ideal - proceed to sleep |
| 150-200 mg/dL | Monitor; check with doctor if persistent |
| > 200 mg/dL | Speak with healthcare team about adjustment |

*Always follow your personal targets from your healthcare provider*

## Foods That Help Sleep

### The Sleep-Promoting Diabetes-Friendly Foods

**1. Tryptophan-Rich Foods**
- Turkey breast (3 oz): 0g carbs, promotes serotonin
- Eggs: High in tryptophan, low glycemic impact
- Seeds (pumpkin, chia): Fiber + tryptophan

**2. Magnesium-Rich Foods**
- Almonds (1 oz): 6g carbs, high magnesium
- Spinach: Nearly 0 carbs, muscle relaxation
- Dark chocolate (1 oz, 85%+): 13g carbs, magnesium + relaxation

**3. Complex Carbs (Small Portions)**
- Steel-cut oatmeal (small serving): Helps tryptophan reach brain
- Sweet potato (small): Potassium for muscle relaxation
- Chickpeas: Vitamin B6 for melatonin production

**4. Sleep-Promoting Beverages**
- Chamomile tea: 0 carbs, reduces anxiety
- Tart cherry juice (4 oz): Natural melatonin (count the carbs!)
- Warm almond milk: Low carb, calming

### The Ideal Diabetic Bedtime Snack (If Needed)

**Recipe: Sleep-Promoting Snack**
- 1 oz cheese or nut butter
- 2-3 whole grain crackers OR apple slices
- Chamomile tea

**Total:** ~15g carbs, balanced with protein/fat

## Foods That Hurt Sleep

### Foods to Avoid Within 3-4 Hours of Bedtime

**1. High-Sugar Foods**
- Candy, cookies, desserts
- Sweetened beverages
- Fruit juice
- **Why:** Blood sugar spikes â†’ crashes â†’ wake up hungry/sweaty

**2. Caffeine Sources**
- Coffee (obvious)
- Tea (except herbal)
- Dark chocolate (more than 1 oz)
- Some medications (check labels)
- **Half-life:** 5-6 hours; avoid after 2 PM

**3. Alcohol**
- Disrupts REM sleep
- Causes blood sugar instability
- Increases urination/dehydration
- **If you drink:** Stop 3+ hours before bed

**4. Heavy/Fatty Meals**
- Takes longer to digest
- Can cause acid reflux
- Diverts energy to digestion vs. repair

**5. Spicy Foods**
- Raises body temperature
- Can cause heartburn
- Disrupts sleep architecture

**6. High-Protein-Only Meals**
- Hard to digest
- May increase alertness
- **Balance with small amount of carbs if eating before bed**

### Hidden Sleep Disruptors

| Food/Drink | Hidden Issue |
|------------|--------------|
| Sugar-free gum | Sorbitol can cause GI distress |
| Tomato sauce | Acidic, causes reflux |
| Aged cheese | Tyramine increases alertness |
| Processed meats | Sodium causes thirst/waking |

## Sleep Apnea Warning Signs

### Why This Matters for Diabetics

**Shocking Statistics:**
- 50-80% of Type 2 diabetics have sleep apnea
- 7x more common in diabetics than general population
- Often undiagnosed for years
- Treating sleep apnea can reduce A1C by 0.5-1%

### Additional Warning Signs

**Nighttime Symptoms:**
- â˜ Loud, frequent snoring
- â˜ Gasping or choking during sleep
- â˜ Restless sleep
- â˜ Frequent urination (nocturia)
- â˜ Night sweats

- â˜ Excessive daytime sleepiness

- â˜ Difficulty concentrating
- â˜ Memory problems
- â˜ Irritability/mood changes
- â˜ Difficulty losing weight despite efforts

### When to Seek Help

**Talk to your doctor if you have:**
- 3+ STOP-BANG risk factors
- Bed partner reports breathing pauses
- Persistently high morning glucose despite good evening numbers
- Unexplained fatigue despite "adequate" sleep

**Testing Options:**
- Home sleep apnea test (HSAT)
- In-lab polysomnography
- Ask your endocrinologist for referral

## 30-Day Sleep-Glucose Tracker

### How to Use This Tracker

**Daily Recording (5 minutes):**
1. Each morning, record last night's sleep data
2. Record morning fasting glucose
3. Note any factors that might have affected both
4. Rate your energy level

**Weekly Review (15 minutes):**
1. Calculate your averages
2. Look for patterns
3. Identify what's working/not working

### Tracking Key

**Sleep Quality Rating (1-5):**
- 1 = Terrible (multiple long awakenings, feel exhausted)
- 2 = Poor (woke several times, don't feel rested)
- 3 = Fair (some interruptions, somewhat rested)
- 4 = Good (minimal interruptions, feel rested)
- 5 = Excellent (slept through, feel refreshed)

**Energy Level (1-5):**
- 1 = Exhausted, can barely function
- 2 = Very tired, struggling
- 3 = Somewhat tired, managing
- 4 = Good energy, productive
- 5 = Excellent, full of energy
