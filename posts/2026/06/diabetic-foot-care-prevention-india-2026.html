Diabetic Foot Care: Prevention Guide for Indians 2026

With diabetes prevalence rising rapidly in India, the risk of foot complications like ulcers and amputations has become a critical public health concern. This guide outlines evidence-based prevention strategies tailored to the Indian lifestyle, helping patients protect their mobility and quality of life through daily vigilance and professional care.

The Rising Burden of Foot Complications in India

Diabetes mellitus is often described as a systemic disease, but its impact on the feet can be particularly devastating and irreversible. In India, the situation is unique due to a combination of genetic predisposition, dietary habits, and the late stage at which many patients seek medical attention. According to the International Diabetes Federation (IDF) Diabetes Atlas, India currently has one of the highest numbers of adults living with diabetes globally, with estimates suggesting over 100 million cases. This demographic reality places a significant strain on the healthcare system, particularly regarding chronic complications. The primary threat to diabetic feet is not just high blood sugar itself, but the long-term damage it inflicts on nerves and blood vessels. Peripheral neuropathy, a condition where nerve damage leads to loss of sensation, affects approximately 50% of people with long-standing diabetes, as noted in studies published in *The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology*. When sensation is lost, minor injuries go unnoticed, leading to infections that can rapidly progress to gangrene. Furthermore, peripheral arterial disease (PAD) restricts blood flow, hindering the body’s natural ability to heal wounds. The combination of these factors creates a perfect storm where a small blister or cut can escalate into a severe ulcer requiring hospitalization.

Daily Inspection: The First Line of Defense

The most effective strategy for preventing foot ulcers is consistent, daily self-examination. Because neuropathy dulls the ability to feel pain, visual inspection becomes the primary tool for detecting early signs of trouble. Patients are advised to check their feet every evening, ideally in a well-lit room. This routine should be non-negotiable, regardless of how busy the day has been. A thorough inspection involves looking at the tops, bottoms, sides, and between the toes. Patients should specifically look for:
  • Cuts, scrapes, or blisters that may have occurred during the day.
  • Redness, swelling, or warmth, which are early signs of inflammation or infection.
  • Corn calluses, which can break down and ulcerate if left untreated.
  • Dryness or cracking skin, which can serve as entry points for bacteria.
  • Changes in nail color or shape, such as ingrown toenails or fungal infections.
For patients who cannot see the bottom of their feet due to limited mobility or vision impairment, using a mirror or asking a family member for assistance is crucial. If any abnormality is found, it should be cleaned gently with mild soap and water, and a healthcare provider should be consulted immediately, rather than attempting home remedies with unsterile instruments.

Footwear and Environmental Hazards

In the Indian context, footwear choices play a pivotal role in foot health. The traditional practice of walking barefoot or wearing open-toed chappals at home exposes feet to numerous hazards. From a clinical perspective, the American Diabetes Association (ADA) strongly recommends that diabetic patients never walk barefoot, even indoors. This is because minor punctures from thorns, glass, or rough flooring can go unnoticed and lead to severe infection. Selecting the right footwear requires attention to fit, material, and design. Shoes should have a wide toe box to prevent crowding of the toes, which can lead to deformities like hammertoes. The material should be breathable, such as leather or mesh, to reduce moisture buildup that encourages fungal growth. It is advisable to buy shoes in the evening when feet are slightly swollen to ensure a proper fit throughout the day. Additionally, patients should check the inside of their shoes before wearing them. Small objects like pebbles, stones, or rough seams can cause significant friction and blisters. Wearing socks made of cotton or moisture-wicking synthetic blends can help maintain dryness. However, socks with tight elastic bands should be avoided as they can restrict circulation. For patients with existing foot deformities or a history of ulcers, custom-made orthopedic footwear may be necessary, as recommended by podiatrists.

Professional Care and Glycemic Control

While daily self-care is essential, professional intervention is equally important. Regular visits to a podiatrist or a healthcare provider trained in diabetic foot care can help identify issues before they become serious. The ADA recommends a comprehensive foot exam at least once a year for all patients, and more frequently for those with risk factors such as previous ulcers, amputation, or severe neuropathy. During these exams, providers may use tools like monofilament testing to assess sensation and check pulses to evaluate blood flow. Glycemic control remains the cornerstone of prevention. High blood sugar levels accelerate nerve damage and impair immune function, making infections harder to treat. The Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) and the Association of Indian Diabetologists (AID) generally recommend maintaining HbA1c levels below 7% for most non-pregnant adults, although individual targets may vary based on age and comorbidities. Achieving this target reduces the risk of microvascular complications, including neuropathy. Furthermore, lifestyle modifications such as a balanced diet rich in fiber and low in refined carbohydrates, along with regular physical activity, can improve circulation and overall foot health. Walking, for instance, promotes blood flow to the lower extremities, but patients must ensure their footwear is supportive and that they do not overexert themselves to the point of injury.

When to Seek Immediate Medical Attention

Despite best efforts, complications can arise. Recognizing the signs of a serious infection or ischemia is critical for saving the foot. Patients should seek immediate medical attention if they experience:
  • A wound that does not heal within two weeks.
  • Signs of infection, such as pus, foul odor, or increasing redness spreading from the site.
  • Severe pain or sudden onset of coldness in the foot, which may indicate acute arterial blockage.
  • Blackening of the skin (gangrene), which signifies tissue death.
Early intervention can often prevent the need for amputation. Diabetic foot ulcers are a leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputations globally, but they are largely preventable with consistent care and education. By integrating these practices into daily life, Indian patients can significantly reduce their risk of severe complications. Consult your doctor for personalized advice on managing your diabetes and protecting your feet.
